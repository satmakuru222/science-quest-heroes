Space Concepts

What Is the Sun?

The Sun is a giant ball of hot gas (mostly hydrogen and helium) that provides light and heat to our entire Solar System!

Key Facts:
  • ‚òÄÔ∏è Temperature: 27 million¬∞F at core
  • üìè Size: 109 times wider than Earth
  • ‚ö° Energy: Nuclear fusion reactions
  • üí° Age: 4.6 billion years old
Why It Matters:
  • üåç Provides warmth for life on Earth
  • üå± Powers plant growth (photosynthesis)
  • üåä Drives Earth's weather and water cycle
  • ü™ê Gravity holds all planets in orbit
What is an Orbit?

An orbit is the curved path a planet takes around the Sun, caused by the Sun's gravity pulling on the planet.

üåç Revolution

When a planet travels around the Sun (makes one complete orbit = one year for that planet)

üîÑ Rotation

When a planet spins on its axis (one complete spin = one day for that planet)

Fun Fact: Neptune takes 165 Earth years to orbit the Sun once! Since it was discovered in 1846, it hasn't even completed two full orbits yet!

Mission 1: Build a Solar System Model

Materials Needed

Styrofoam balls (various sizes) Paint Wire or string Cardboard base

Procedure

  1. Choose appropriate ball sizes (Jupiter biggest, Mercury smallest)
  2. Paint each planet with accurate colors
  3. Arrange planets in correct order from the Sun
  4. Label each planet with fun facts
üìè

Mission 2: Scale Distance Activity

Materials Needed

Measuring tape Markers or flags Large outdoor space

Procedure

  1. Mark the Sun position (starting point)
  2. Use scale: 1 meter = 10 million km
  3. Place planet markers at scaled distances
  4. Walk the "orbit" to understand vastness of space!
üî≠

Mission 3: Planet Observation Journal

Materials Needed

Notebook Binoculars or telescope (optional) Star chart or app

Procedure

  1. Find visible planets in night sky (Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn)
  2. Record date, time, and location of observation
  3. Draw what you see and note brightness/color
  4. Track how planets move over several nights
